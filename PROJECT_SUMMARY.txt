- Status: Requires gain reduction for digital implementation

KEY INSIGHTS:
1. Digital implementation introduces significant phase lag at high frequencies
2. Type II compensator with low crossover is stable for digital use
3. Type III compensator with high crossover becomes unstable digitally
4. Phase loss is frequency-dependent: higher fc = more phase loss
5. Design must account for Hdig, not just Hc, for digital controllers

ERICKSON VS REFERENCE PAPER COMPARISON
===============================================================================

ERICKSON'S APPROACH:
- Systematic, analytical methodology
- K-factor method for optimal pole-zero placement
- Clear design guidelines based on control theory
- Predictable performance across topologies
- Best for: New designs, rigorous optimization

REFERENCE PAPER APPROACH:
- Empirical, application-specific tuning
- Based on measured response and rules of thumb
- Faster iteration for similar designs
- Variable mathematical rigor
- Best for: Adapting existing designs, quick prototyping

RECOMMENDATION:
Use Erickson's systematic approach for robust, predictable designs.
Supplement with practical insights from reference papers.
Always verify with simulation before hardware implementation.

===============================================================================
Hc vs Hdig COMPARISON SUMMARY
===============================================================================

CONTINUOUS COMPENSATION (Hc):
- Theoretical ideal
- No sampling delays
- Infinite update rate
- Benchmark for comparison

DIGITAL COMPENSATION (Hdig):
- Real-world implementation: ADC → PID → DAC
- Includes sampling delay: ~1.5 sample periods
- Zero-Order Hold (ZOH) effect from DAC
- Additional phase lag at high frequencies

PHASE DEGRADATION FORMULA:
ΔPhase ≈ -360° × fc × Td - (fc/fs) × 30°

For fc = 10 kHz, fs = 100 kHz, Td = 1.5×Ts:
ΔPhase ≈ -54° - 3° = -57°

DESIGN IMPLICATIONS FOR DIGITAL:
1. Reduce crossover frequency: fc,digital < fc,analog
2. Target fc < fs/6 for good stability
3. Increase sampling frequency if possible
4. Add extra phase boost in compensator design
5. Always simulate with Hdig, not just Hc
